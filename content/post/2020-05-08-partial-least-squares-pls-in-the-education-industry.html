For the discussion topic this week, I will discuss three ways that partial least squares (PLS) are used within the education industry to aid in decision-making. Also, I will discuss some of the reasoning to use PLS regression instead of standard regression models.

Suleiman Y. (2016) conducted a study that investigates how student personnel services affect academic achievement in secondary schools in Kwara State, Nigeria. The study involved a questionnaire distributed to 379 students, which recorded their responses on a four-point Likert scale (1-4), with reactions ranging from ‘strongly disagree’ to ‘strongly agree.’ Additionally, the students’ grades in five subjects (Math, English, Biology, Geography, and Economics) were used to determine academic achievement.

The study examined two independent variables, Admission Services, and Extracurricular Services, to determine their effect on the outcome variable, Academic Achievement. Figure 1 displays the conceptual framework of the study.

Reprinted from “Relationship between Admission, Extracurricular Services and Academic Achievement in Secondary Schools: A Partial Least Square (PLS) Approach,” by Suleiman et al., 2016, Journal of Education and Practice, 7(8), p. 32.

Figure 2 displays students’ perceptions of admission services, while Figure 3 shows students’ perceptions of extracurricular services.

Figure 2. Students’ perception of admission services. Reprinted from “Relationship between Admission, Extracurricular Services and Academic Achievement in Secondary Schools: A Partial Least Square (PLS) Approach,” by Suleiman et al., 2016, Journal of Education and Practice, 7(8), p. 33.

Figure 3. Students’ perception of extracurricular services. Reprinted from “Relationship between Admission, Extracurricular Services and Academic Achievement in Secondary Schools: A Partial Least Square (PLS) Approach,” by Suleiman et al., 2016, Journal of Education and Practice, 7(8), p. 33.

The results indicate that students had a moderate level of perception of admission services, while students had a high opinion of extracurricular activities. The results found a significant relationship between admission services and academic achievement, as well as a significant relationship between extracurricular activities (e.g., volleyball, basketball, and badminton) and academic achievement. Admission services allow schools to identify qualified candidates for correct placement within the school system (Suleiman Y. 2016).

Consequently, this study presents two ways in which PLS is used within the education industry to affect decision making:

  • Is there a relationship between admission services and academic achievement?
  • Is there a relationship between extracurricular services and academic achievement? (Suleiman Y. 2016).

A third way we can use PLS to affect decision making within education is to determine whether these independent variables have any effect on retention rates, graduation rates, or persistence. Additionally, we might consider other independent variables, such as the first-generation statuses of the students.

Lastly, PLS regression allows for a small number of variables within the data to explain a lot of the variation. PLS regression accounts for variation in both input variables and target variables. Some original variables, not constructed components, are kept as inputs, and categorical input variables can be used (Georges J. 2017). Moreover, Suleiman Y. (2016) describe the software configuration of their study as being “free from any postulation in terms of multicollinearity, sample size, normality, missing values, etc.” (p. 32).

In conclusion, we have discussed three ways PLS can benefit decision making in education, and some of the benefits PLS regression offers over standard regression models.
